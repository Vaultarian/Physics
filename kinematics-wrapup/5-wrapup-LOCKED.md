# AP Physics — Kinematic Wrap Up
**Physics Kahuna · c. 2001 · review & AP exam prep**

---

## AP Exam Kinematic Equations

These are the only equations provided on the AP exam:

$$v = v_0 + at$$

$$x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$$

$$v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)$$

Note: the last is the same as $v_{\text{avg}} = \frac{v + v_0}{2}$ combined with the displacement equation.

That's it. That's all you get.

## I. Displacement and Velocity Vectors

**You should know how to deal with displacement and velocity vectors so you can:**

### a) Relate velocity, displacement, and time for constant velocity

*Okay, this is easy stuff. We did us a bunch of problems and stuff on this. Basically you use the $x/t$ equation. Note that you don't seem to have it in the list above. But you do! It's the $x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ one. See, the acceleration is zero because you have a constant velocity, so all the terms with acceleration in them cancel out. $x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ becomes $x = x_0 + v_0 t$ — if the initial displacement is zero, then we get $x = v_0 t$ — okay?*

### b) Resolve a vector into components along two perpendicular axes

*We did a bunch of this. We learned how to use the sine and cosine function to resolve a vector into its components.*

### c) Add vectors to find net displacement

*This is another skill that you have worked hard to develop. Resolve the vectors into components, add up the x and y components and then solve for the resultant vector. You probably got sick of doing problems like this. Usually, in the test, this will be part of a question that is really dealing with something else. For example, you might have to find two electric force vectors and then add them up to find the resultant.*

### d) Subtract displacement vectors

*This sounds horribly difficult, but it's just a simple business with adding vectors.*

## III. AP Exam Examples

### 1998 AP Physics — Question 1

**Problem:** Two small blocks, each of mass ***m***, are connected by a string of constant length 4***h*** and negligible mass. Block ***A*** is placed on a smooth tabletop as shown below, and block ***B*** hangs over the edge of the table. The tabletop is a distance 2***h*** above the floor. Block ***B*** is then released from rest at a distance ***h*** above the floor at time ***t*** = 0.

[Figure: block A on smooth tabletop, block B hanging over edge; string length 4h; table height 2h; block B starts at height h above floor — see index.html]

**(a)** Determine the acceleration of block ***B*** as it descends.

**(b)** Block ***B*** strikes the floor and does not bounce. Determine the time ***t***₁ at which block ***B*** strikes the floor.

**(c)** Describe the motion of block ***A*** from time ***t*** = 0 to the time when block ***B*** strikes the floor.

**(d)** Describe the motion of block ***A*** from the time block ***B*** strikes the floor to the time block ***A*** leaves the table.

**(e)** Determine the distance between the landing points of the two blocks.

The main thrust of the question is to look at Newton's laws (which we will soon get into). You use Newton's laws and forces to figure out the acceleration in part a.

Part b does deal with the stuff you've learned. You use $h = \frac{1}{2}at^2$ to figure out the time.

Parts c and d are Newton's law deals. We'll learn to do these later.

Part e you can do — block ***B*** falls straight down, block ***A*** slides off the table with some horizontal velocity. When it slides off the table it is a projectile.

**Solution (b):** Acceleration found from Newton's laws (part a):

$$a = \frac{g}{2}$$

Block B falls distance $h$:

$$h = \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 \implies t^2 = \frac{2h}{a} = \frac{2h}{g/2} = \frac{4h}{g}$$

$$t_1 = 2\sqrt{\frac{h}{g}}$$

**Solution (e):** Block A accelerates over distance $3h$:

$$v^2 = 2a(3h) = 2\!\cdot\!\frac{g}{2}\!\cdot\!3h = 3gh \implies v = \sqrt{3gh}$$

Block A falls distance $2h$ from tabletop:

$$2h = \tfrac{1}{2}g\,t^2 \implies t = 2\sqrt{\frac{h}{g}} = t_1$$

Horizontal distance of block A from table edge:

$$x_A = v\cdot t = \sqrt{3gh}\cdot 2\sqrt{\frac{h}{g}} = 2h\sqrt{3}$$

Block B falls straight down (no horizontal displacement). Distance between landing points:

$$\Delta x = 2h\sqrt{3}$$

### 1994 AP Physics — Ball Kicked Toward Fence

**Problem:** A ball of mass 0.5 kg, initially at rest, is kicked directly toward a fence from a point 32 m away. The velocity of the ball as it leaves the kicker's foot is 20 m/s at an angle of 37° above the horizontal. The top of the fence is 2.5 m high. The kicker's foot is in contact with the ball for 0.05 s. Air resistance is negligible.

[Figure: ball kicked at 37°, 20 m/s toward fence 32 m away, fence height 2.5 m — see index.html]

**(a)** Determine the magnitude of the average net force exerted on the ball during the kick.

*This is stuff you don't know how to do yet (but you will).*

**(b)** Determine the time it takes for the ball to reach the plane of the fence.

**Solution (b):** *We know that the horizontal velocity is constant.*

$$v_x = v\cos\theta = 20\cos 37° = 16\ \text{m/s}$$

$$t = \frac{x}{v_x} = \frac{32}{16} = 2.0\ \text{s}$$

**(c)** Will the ball hit the fence? If so, how far below the top? If not, how far above?

**Solution (c):** Find vertical position at $t = 2.0$ s:

$$y = v_{0y}\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 = (20\sin 37°)(2.0) + \tfrac{1}{2}(-9.8)(2.0)^2$$

$$y = (12)(2.0) - 19.6 = 24 - 19.6 = 4.4\ \text{m}$$

Since 4.4 m > 2.5 m, the ball clears the fence. Height above fence:

$$4.4 - 2.5 = 1.9\ \text{m above the top of the fence}$$

**(d)** Sketch the horizontal and vertical components of velocity as functions of time.

*The idea here is that the horizontal velocity is constant, but that the vertical velocity is changing. The lower graph shows this — the velocity is a straight line. The point where it crosses the x axis and becomes negative is, of course, the highest point on its path where its velocity is zero.*

[Figure: vx vs t (flat line); vy vs t (decreasing linear, crosses zero at apex) — see index.html]
